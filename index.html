Peer-Reviewed Publications

  1. Electoral Predictors of Polling Errors. (with Sina Chen, John Kortner, and Peter Selb). The Journal of Politics, Forthcoming
    2. Abstract

    Case studies of polling failures focus on within-election differences in poll accuracy. The crucial question of why polls fail in one election but not in others often remains a matter of speculation. To develop a contextual understanding, we review and unify theories of election features suspected of encouraging polling errors, including mobilization, candidacies, polarization, and electoral conduct. We extend a Bayesian hierarchical modeling approach that separates poll bias and variance at the election level and links error components to electoral predictors. Investigating 6,375 pre-election polls across 318 U.S. Senate elections, 1990-2022, we find an overall trend toward smaller but more uniform errors. Poll variance exhibits a weak negative association with mobilization and polarization. Until 2004, frontrunners and incumbents were overestimated, but there is little evidence that polls are biased for female or minority candidates. Finally, Republicans in states with lower levels of state democracy are slightly underestimated in recent years.

Working Papers

  1. Selection and Measurement Biases in Survey Research on Political Participation: Theory and Evidence. (with Peter Selb). American Political Science Review, R&R
    2. Abstract

    Surveys are vital for studying political participation but often face two key challenges: individuals who engage in politics are overrepresented in surveys, and participation is overreported by respondents. Both phenomena inflate marginal survey estimates. But how do they affect inferences about social differences in political participation? Extant research does not provide a clear answer. We employ directed acyclic graphs to theoretically determine potential biases. We then use validated votes and social characteristics of both respondents and nonrespondents from the Swedish National Election Studies 1998–2022 to empirically assess biases in estimated turnout disparities across age groups, gender, education, income, nativity status, and place of residence. We find that both selection and measurement biases have been rising and often reinforcing each other to attenuate social differences in turnout. We discuss implications for survey research on political participation and beyond.

  2. Has Survey Data Quality Deteriorated? Evidence from Eight Decades of Vote Validation. (with Peter Selb). Working Paper
    3. Abstract

    Declining response rates, eroding coverage, and growing reliance on opt-in panels for respondent recruitment have raised concerns about deteriorating survey data quality. However, the empirical assessment of survey errors is usually hampered by the absence of gold standard measures against which survey responses and statistics can be validated, let alone over longer time periods. We assembled a collection of 91 vote validation studies from nine countries, 1956–2024, to disentangle error components and track their time trends and cross-study patterns. Our findings suggest that representational errors have been on the rise, a trend presumably related to declining response rates and greater use of opt-in panels. Measurement error, conversely, shows a slight downward trend, possibly due to shifting survey modes that diminish normative pressure to misreport. We discuss the implications of these and other findings for survey-based research on electoral behavior and beyond.

  3. Electing the Least Preferred: Evidence of Condorcet Losers in Germany. Working Paper
    4. Abstract

    Ensuring that elected representatives reflect majority preferences is a central challenge in first-past-the-post electoral systems, motivating reforms such as mixed-member and ranked-choice voting. Yet such institutional designs can generate new distortions at the district level. I examine this possibility in Germany’s mixed-member proportional system using the Condorcet criterion, which identifies as most representative the candidate who would defeat all others in pairwise contests. Leveraging pre-election survey data on candidate and party evaluations, I simulate hypothetical head-to-head contests among district candidates and aggregate predicted choices to the population of actual voters in each constituency. Across the 2021 and 2025 Bundestag elections, a pronounced divergence between plurality outcomes and Condorcet-consistent outcomes emerges. In 2021, all sixteen AfD district winners are highly likely Condorcet losers, while many unelected Social Democratic candidates are likely Condorcet winners. The pattern intensifies in 2025, with all forty-six AfD district winners appearing to be Condorcet losers and many CDU/CSU candidates estimated to be Condorcet winners. These findings indicate that mixed-member systems can generate district outcomes in which plurality winners are not the candidates who would receive majority support in pairwise comparisons.

  4. Where are the Moderates? Working Paper
    5. Abstract

    Do Americans hold substantively moderate policy preferences, or does apparent moderation reflect the limits of contemporary survey-based measurement? Using panel data from the 2020--2024 Cooperative Election Study, this paper evaluates whether ideological centrality corresponds to coherent and stable policy positions. I estimate a hierarchical dynamic latent-variable model that separates global ideological placement from heterogeneity, instability, idiosyncrasy, and unsystematic responding. The results replicate the familiar finding that many respondents appear ideologically central, but show that centrality alone rarely corresponds to substantively moderate preference structures. Instead, apparent moderation reflects multiple underlying processes, and estimates of a moderate electorate depend critically on how coherence and stability are defined. These findings caution against inferring widespread policy centrism from one-dimensional or classification-based summaries and clarify the measurement conditions under which claims about moderation are empirically warranted.

  5. The Predictive Role of Covariate Shift in Survey Inference. Early-Stage Concept
    6. Abstract

    This is an early-stage concept that draws on recent work on covariate shift and distributional robustness, including Jin, Egami, and Rothenhäusler (2025), and explores its implications for survey inference.

Other Work

  1. Vereinfachter Zugang zur Grundsicherung: Wer von einer Schonfrist bei Vermögensanrechnung und Aufwendungen für die Unterkunft profitieren würde. (with Jonas Beste and Mark Trappmann). IAB-Forum, 2021
    2. Abstract

    Derzeit wird in der Politik viel über einen vereinfachten Zugang zu Leistungen der Grundsicherung diskutiert. Von einer Karenzzeit für die Prüfung von Vermögen und Angemessenheit der Wohnung würden insbesondere Personen profitieren, die über eine abgeschlossene Berufsausbildung und über eine längere Erwerbshistorie verfügen. Das käme dem politischen Ziel entgegen, Lebensleistung noch stärker zu honorieren und so den Abstiegsängsten der Mittelschichten entgegenzuwirken.
